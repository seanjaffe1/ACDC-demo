\begin{abstract}
We present an SE(3)-equivariant flow matching model for generative cardiac
shape modelling directly on surface meshes.
Given registered left-ventricular meshes extracted from the ACDC cine-MRI
dataset, our model learns a conditional velocity field over mesh vertex
positions using an $E(n)$ Equivariant Graph Neural Network (EGNN), trained
with the optimal-transport conditional flow matching objective.
A VAE-style encoder compresses each mesh into a 64-dimensional latent code
that captures pathology-relevant shape variation.
On reconstruction quality, our model achieves a Chamfer distance of
$0.968 \pm 0.078$~mm and Hausdorff distance of $1.576 \pm 0.094$~mm,
an 8.7$\times$ improvement in Chamfer distance over the PCA shape model
baseline ($8.40 \pm 1.04$~mm).
In distribution quality, 1-NNA of $0.600$ indicates generated shapes are
partially indistinguishable from real data, and a linear probe on the latent
space achieves $33\%$ accuracy across five unsupervised pathology classes
(chance: $20\%$), demonstrating that the latent space encodes
clinically meaningful structure without label supervision.
We further introduce a gauge-equivariant encoder augmented with per-vertex
surface curvature features ($\kappa_1, \kappa_2, H, K$).
With KL warmup, the gauge encoder achieves 1-NNA $0.590$ and reduces
clinical EF error from $230\%$ to $157\%$.
Adding geometric coordinate pooling and attention-weighted aggregation
further raises pathology probe accuracy to $\mathbf{41\%}$, the best
across all tested configurations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Cardiac shape is an established biomarker for a range of cardiovascular
diseases, including dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM), hypertrophic
cardiomyopathy (HCM), and myocardial infarction (MINF).
The ability to generate realistic, pathology-conditioned cardiac shape
samples is valuable both for data augmentation in clinical machine learning
pipelines and for studying the morphological variability associated with
specific disease phenotypes.

The dominant approach to statistical cardiac shape modelling is PCA applied
to registered point correspondences~\cite{cootes1995active}.
While computationally efficient, PCA captures only linear variation and
struggles to represent the non-linear deformation modes characteristic of
asymmetric remodelling (e.g.\ septal thickening in HCM).
Deep generative models address this limitation, but most operate on
voxel grids~\cite{song2020score}, which discard the surface topology
of cardiac structures and scale poorly with spatial resolution.
Mesh-based representations preserve topology and operate directly in
physical millimetre coordinates, yet pose challenges for standard neural
network architectures because they are not invariant to rigid-body
transformations of the input.

We present an \emph{SE(3)-equivariant flow matching model} for generative
cardiac shape modelling directly on surface meshes.
Our encoder uses an $E(n)$ Equivariant Graph Neural Network
(EGNN)~\cite{satorras2021n} to compress each mesh into a 64-dimensional
latent code, and a separate EGNN velocity field is trained with the
Optimal Transport conditional flow matching objective~\cite{lipman2022flow}
to generate new meshes by integrating the learned transport from noise to
data.
By construction, the entire pipeline is equivariant to $\SE$: a rotation
of the input mesh produces an identically rotated output, eliminating the
need for data augmentation to handle pose variation.

On the ACDC dataset of 100 cine-MRI subjects spanning five pathology classes,
our model achieves Chamfer distance $0.968 \pm 0.078$~mm and Hausdorff
distance $1.576 \pm 0.094$~mm on reconstruction, an $8.7\times$ improvement
over the PCA baseline ($8.40 \pm 1.04$~mm Chamfer).
Generated shapes attain a 1-NNA score of 0.600, partially indistinguishable
from real data, and a linear pathology probe on the latent space achieves
33\% accuracy across five classes (chance: 20\%), demonstrating that the
model captures clinically meaningful shape variation without label
supervision.
We further propose a gauge-equivariant encoder variant that incorporates
per-vertex surface curvature.
With KL warmup, this achieves 1-NNA $0.590$ and reduces EF error from
$230\%$ to $157\%$; augmenting the pooling with geometric coordinate
features and per-node attention raises pathology probe accuracy to
$41\%$---the best across all configurations tested.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Statistical Shape Models}
\label{sec:bg_ssm}

Statistical shape models (SSMs) represent shape variation within a
population by applying principal component analysis (PCA) to a set of
registered point correspondences~\cite{cootes1995active}.
Given $N$ shapes with $V$ vertices each, we flatten each shape to a
vector $\mathbf{s}_i \in \R^{3V}$ and compute the empirical covariance.
New shapes are sampled as
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{s}} = \bar{\mathbf{s}} + \mathbf{P}\mathbf{b},
    \quad \mathbf{b} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Lambda}),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\mathbf{s}}$ is the mean shape, $\mathbf{P}$ are the
principal modes, and $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is the diagonal matrix of
eigenvalues.
SSMs are computationally efficient but capture only linear variation,
failing to represent non-linear deformation modes such as the asymmetric
remodelling seen in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.

\subsection{Geometric Deep Learning and SE(3) Equivariance}
\label{sec:bg_geo}

A function $f$ is \emph{equivariant} under a symmetry group $G$ if
\begin{equation}
    f(g \cdot \mathbf{x}) = g \cdot f(\mathbf{x})
    \quad \forall\, g \in G,\; \mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}.
\end{equation}
For 3-D point sets, the relevant group is $\SE = \SO \ltimes \R^3$,
the group of rotations and translations in three dimensions.
A neural network operating on point positions that is equivariant to
$\SE$ will produce geometrically consistent outputs regardless of the
coordinate frame in which the input is expressed.

We use the \emph{$E(n)$ Equivariant Graph Neural Network} (EGNN)
of~\citet{satorras2021n} as our equivariant backbone.
Each EGNN layer jointly updates scalar node features $\mathbf{h}_i$
and equivariant coordinates $\mathbf{x}_i$:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{m}_{ij} &= \phi_e\!\left(\mathbf{h}_i,\, \mathbf{h}_j,\,
                       \norm{\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j}^2\right),
    \label{eq:egnn_msg} \\
    \mathbf{x}_i    &\leftarrow \mathbf{x}_i
                       + \sum_{j \neq i}
                         (\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j)\,
                         \phi_x(\mathbf{m}_{ij}),
    \label{eq:egnn_coord} \\
    \mathbf{h}_i    &\leftarrow \phi_h\!\left(\mathbf{h}_i,\,
                       \sum_{j}\mathbf{m}_{ij}\right),
    \label{eq:egnn_node}
\end{align}
where $\phi_e$, $\phi_x$, and $\phi_h$ are multi-layer perceptrons.
Because the coordinate update in~\eqref{eq:egnn_coord} depends only on
relative positions and the scalar $\phi_x$, the overall mapping
$(\mathbf{H}, \mathbf{X}) \mapsto (\mathbf{H}', \mathbf{X}')$ is
equivariant to $\SE$ by construction.

\subsection{Diffusion Models and Flow Matching}
\label{sec:bg_flow}

Score-based diffusion models~\cite{song2020score} learn to reverse a
forward noising process, but typically require thousands of denoising
steps at inference time.
\emph{Flow matching}~\cite{lipman2022flow} instead defines a
deterministic probability path between a noise distribution $p_0$ and
the data distribution $p_1$, parameterising the corresponding velocity
field $v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ that transports samples from $p_0$
to $p_1$.

Under the \emph{Optimal Transport} (OT) path~\cite{albergo2023building},
interpolating trajectories are straight lines:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_t = (1 - t)\,\mathbf{x}_0 + t\,\mathbf{x}_1,
    \quad t \sim \mathcal{U}[0, 1],
\end{equation}
yielding the simple regression objective
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_\text{CFM}(\theta)
    = \mathbb{E}_{t,\, \mathbf{x}_0 \sim p_0,\, \mathbf{x}_1 \sim p_1}
      \left[ \norm{v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{z})
             - (\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0)}^2 \right],
    \label{eq:cfm_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{z}$ is an optional conditioning variable.
Samples are generated by integrating $v_\theta$ from $t=0$ to $t=1$
using a standard ODE solver (Euler or RK4).
Compared to score-based methods, flow matching is faster to train
(single forward pass per sample) and requires far fewer integration
steps at inference.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}
\label{sec:method_data}

We use the \emph{Automated Cardiac Diagnosis Challenge} (ACDC)
dataset~\cite{bernard2018deep}, which provides cine-MRI volumes with
expert segmentation masks for 100 subjects spanning five pathology
classes: normal (NOR), myocardial infarction (MINF), dilated
cardiomyopathy (DCM), hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), and right
ventricular abnormality (RV).
Segmentations label the left ventricle (LV, label~3), right ventricle
(RV cavity, label~1), and myocardium (Myo, label~2) at end-diastole
(ED) and end-systole (ES).

\paragraph{Mesh extraction.}
For each structure at each frame, we apply the marching cubes
algorithm~\cite{lorensen1987marching} to the binary segmentation mask,
using the physical voxel spacing as the step size to produce meshes in
millimetre coordinates.
We then apply ten iterations of Laplacian smoothing to reduce
staircase artefacts, and decimate each mesh to approximately $2{,}500$
vertices via quadric edge collapse, targeting a balance between
geometric fidelity and computational cost.

\paragraph{Template registration.}
To establish vertex correspondence across subjects we select a single
reference subject and register all others to it.
Registration proceeds in two stages: (i)~a coarse Procrustes
alignment using the Kabsch algorithm, and (ii)~refinement with
Iterative Closest Point (ICP).
After alignment, each mesh is centred at the origin by subtracting
its centroid, removing translation as a degree of freedom.

\paragraph{Graph construction.}
Each registered mesh is stored as a PyTorch Geometric
\texttt{Data} object containing vertex positions
$\mathbf{X} \in \R^{V \times 3}$, a $k$-nearest-neighbour edge index
($k = 8$) computed in the registered coordinate frame, the face
connectivity for surface-area and volume calculations, and an integer
pathology label $y \in \{0, \ldots, 4\}$.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:method_arch}

Our model consists of two learned components: a graph encoder that maps
a cardiac mesh to a latent code, and a flow-matching velocity field
conditioned on that code.

\paragraph{EGNN Encoder.}
The encoder is a four-layer EGNN followed by mean-and-max global
pooling, producing a Gaussian posterior
$q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathcal{G}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},
\text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2))$
with $\boldsymbol{\mu}, \log\boldsymbol{\sigma} \in \R^{64}$.
Initial node features are set to a constant vector
(no semantic node attributes are available), so all information must be
inferred from the geometric structure of the graph.

\paragraph{Equivariant Velocity Field.}
The velocity field $v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{z})$ is a
four-layer EGNN where each node's input feature is the concatenation
of a sinusoidal time embedding and the conditioning code $\mathbf{z}$
projected to the hidden dimension.
The network outputs a velocity vector in $\R^3$ for each vertex.
Because position updates in EGNN depend only on relative coordinates,
the velocity field inherits $\SE$-equivariance: rotating the input
mesh rotates the predicted velocities by the same rotation.

\paragraph{Training Objective.}
The full objective is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{CFM}(\theta)
                + \beta\, D_\text{KL}\!\left(
                    q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathcal{G})
                    \;\|\;
                    \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})
                  \right),
    \label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_\text{CFM}$ is the OT flow matching loss from
Equation~\eqref{eq:cfm_loss} and $\beta$ is a KL weight annealed from
0 to $5 \times 10^{-3}$ over the first 50 epochs.

\subsection{Gauge-Equivariant Encoder}
\label{sec:gauge_method}

To exploit the rich local geometry captured by surface curvature, we
augment the EGNN encoder with a gauge-equivariant message-passing
mechanism.
Each vertex is assigned four scalar curvature features
$(\kappa_1, \kappa_2, H, K)$ computed from the surface mesh (principal
curvature values, mean curvature, and Gaussian curvature).

\paragraph{Equivariant local frame.}
For each vertex $i$, we construct a local-frame vector
$\mathbf{d}_i \in \R^3$ as a learned weighted sum of unit edge vectors:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{d}_i = \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)}
        \alpha_{ij}\,
        \frac{\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i}{\|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\|}}
    {\left\|\sum_{j}\alpha_{ij}\frac{\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i}{\|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\|}\right\|},
    \label{eq:frame}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{ij} = \phi_\alpha(\mathbf{h}_i,\, \kappa_i,\, \|\mathbf{x}_j
- \mathbf{x}_i\|)$ are scalar mixing weights from a small MLP.
Because each term is a scalar times a relative position vector,
$\mathbf{d}_i$ is $\SE$-equivariant: it co-rotates with the point cloud
under any global rotation.

\paragraph{Anisotropic edge weighting.}
Given the local frame, we compute a per-edge anisotropy weight
$w_{ij} \in (0.5, 1.5)$ measuring alignment of each edge with the dominant
curvature direction:
\begin{equation}
    w_{ij} = 0.5 + \phi_w\!\left(
        \left|\frac{\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i}{\|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\|}
        \cdot \mathbf{d}_i\right|,\; \kappa_i
    \right),
    \label{eq:aniso_weight}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_w$ is a sigmoid-normalised MLP.
The absolute-value alignment makes $w_{ij}$ gauge-invariant (independent
of frame orientation).
Edge messages $\mathbf{m}_{ij}$ and coordinate updates are then multiplied
by $w_{ij}$ before aggregation, enabling the encoder to attend
differentially to along-axis vs.\ circumferential edges — which correspond
to clinically distinct modes of LV shape variation.

\paragraph{X-EGNN: explicit curvature eigenvectors.}
The Gauge-EGNN frame $\mathbf{d}_i$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:frame}) is an equivariant
\emph{proxy} for the true curvature directions, constructed from neighbouring
edge vectors.
We propose a stronger variant, \emph{X-EGNN}, that uses the exact
principal-curvature eigenvectors $(\mathbf{p}^1_i, \mathbf{p}^2_i) \in \R^3$
computed by \texttt{libigl} and stored per vertex.
These are the eigenvectors of the shape operator and are $\SE$-equivariant:
they co-rotate with the mesh under any global rotation.

For each edge $(i \to j)$ with unit direction
$\mathbf{e}_{ij} = (\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i)/\|\mathbf{x}_j -
\mathbf{x}_i\|$, we project onto the full 2-D curvature frame:
\begin{equation}
    c^1_{ij} = \mathbf{e}_{ij} \cdot \mathbf{p}^1_i, \qquad
    c^2_{ij} = \mathbf{e}_{ij} \cdot \mathbf{p}^2_i.
    \label{eq:xegnn_proj}
\end{equation}
Because each projection is a dot product of two equivariant vectors it is an
\emph{invariant} scalar.
The anisotropy weight now has access to both projections and their
curvature-weighted counterparts:
\[
    w_{ij} = 0.5 + \phi_w\!\left(
        |c^1_{ij}|,\; |c^2_{ij}|,\;
        c^1_{ij}\kappa^1_i,\; c^2_{ij}\kappa^2_i,\;
        \boldsymbol{\kappa}_i,\; \|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\|
    \right).
\]
The coordinate update is extended from one equivariant term to three:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\mathbf{x}_i = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \Bigl[
        \alpha^\text{base}_{ij}\,
            \tfrac{\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i}{\|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\| + 1}
        + \alpha^1_{ij}\, c^1_{ij}\, \mathbf{p}^1_i
        + \alpha^2_{ij}\, c^2_{ij}\, \mathbf{p}^2_i
    \Bigr],
    \label{eq:xegnn_coord}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha^\text{base}_{ij}, \alpha^1_{ij}, \alpha^2_{ij}$ are
tanh-bounded scalars from independent MLPs applied to the weighted message
$w_{ij}\mathbf{m}_{ij}$.
Each term in Eq.~\ref{eq:xegnn_coord} is equivariant: $c^k_{ij}$ and
$\alpha^k_{ij}$ are invariant scalars, while $\mathbf{p}^k_i$ is equivariant,
so their product co-rotates correctly.
In contrast to Gauge-EGNN, X-EGNN can independently modulate movement
along the maximum- and minimum-curvature directions—a 2-D anisotropy that
matches the intrinsic geometry of the ventricular surface.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:method_baselines}

We compare against four baselines, each designed to ablate a specific
modelling choice.

\textbf{Baseline~1: PCA Shape Model.}
Classical linear SSM fit to flattened vertex positions.
Justifies the use of deep learning at all.

\textbf{Baseline~2: Voxel Diffusion.}
OT flow matching applied to binary segmentation volumes on a $32^3$
grid, using a 3-D U-Net velocity field.
Justifies the mesh representation over voxel grids.

\textbf{Baseline~3: PointNet++ VAE.}
PointNet++ encoder with a FoldingNet decoder trained as a VAE
(no diffusion/flow).
Justifies the distributional modelling component.

\textbf{Baseline~4: Non-equivariant Flow Matching.}
Identical architecture to our main model, but EGNN layers replaced by
standard MLP message passing; random rotation augmentation is applied
during training to compensate.
This is the \emph{critical} ablation: it directly validates the
geometric deep learning contribution.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

All experiments use the ACDC dataset (100 subjects, 5 pathology classes).
We filter to end-diastole left-ventricular meshes and use an 80/20
train/validation split.
The main model is trained for 200 epochs with AdamW (lr $= 3 \times 10^{-4}$,
weight decay $= 10^{-4}$), batch size 8, cosine annealing, and gradient
clipping at 1.0.
The encoder has 4 EGNN layers; the flow model has 6 EGNN layers; hidden
dimension is 128 and latent dimension 64.
KL weight $\beta = 5 \times 10^{-3}$ with linear warmup over the first 50
epochs (from $\beta=0$ to $\beta=5\times10^{-3}$).
These hyperparameters were selected via the ablation study described in
Section~\ref{sec:ablations}.
Training runs on a single NVIDIA GPU.
Full training curves and hyperparameter sweeps are logged to the W\&B project
\texttt{acdc-cardiac-diffusion}.

\subsection{Ablation: Equivariance Under Rotation}

The key architectural claim of our model is that $\SE$-equivariance
improves data efficiency: because the EGNN velocity field generalises
across all rigid-body orientations of the input, it effectively sees
many more training examples per mesh than a non-equivariant counterpart.
Table~\ref{tab:reconstruction} already shows that our model outperforms
the MLP flow matching baseline (B4) on the full training set
(Chamfer 0.968 vs.\ 1.060~mm).
Notably, B4 uses random rotation augmentation during training, yet
the EGNN model achieves lower error without any augmentation, confirming
that baking equivariance into the architecture is more sample-efficient
than augmenting a non-equivariant model.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Ablations}
\label{sec:ablations}

We ablate four design dimensions of the model, each starting from the
baseline configuration (4 flow layers, latent dim 64, $\beta = 10^{-3}$,
no KL warmup) and varying one or two factors at a time.
Results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:ablations}.

\paragraph{KL weight $\beta$.}
Increasing $\beta$ from $10^{-3}$ to $5 \times 10^{-3}$ improves generation
quality (1-NNA from 0.605 to 0.590) at a modest reconstruction cost
(+0.08~mm Chamfer). Doubling further to $10^{-2}$ yields no additional
gain in 1-NNA while further degrading reconstruction, suggesting $5 \times
10^{-3}$ is near-optimal.

\paragraph{KL warmup.}
Linearly ramping $\beta$ from 0 over the first 50 epochs (A2) recovers
most of the reconstruction quality lost by the higher KL weight, reaching
Chamfer $1.011$~mm vs.\ $1.072$~mm without warmup, while retaining the
generation improvement (1-NNA 0.595).

\paragraph{Latent dimension.}
Doubling the latent dimension to 128 (A3) improves pathology probe accuracy
from 32\% to 35\%, confirming the larger code captures more disease-relevant
variation. Reconstruction and 1-NNA are largely unaffected compared to the
baseline, suggesting the bottleneck is not latent capacity per se, but
rather the degree of VAE regularisation.

\paragraph{Flow model depth.}
Increasing the velocity field from 4 to 6 EGNN layers (A4) yields the
best reconstruction (Chamfer $0.967$~mm, $-0.027$~mm vs.\ baseline) but
slightly hurts 1-NNA ($+0.005$), indicating a more expressive velocity
field sharpens shape fidelity at the cost of minor distributional
degradation.

\paragraph{Best configuration.}
Combining KL warmup with 6 flow layers (A2+A4) achieves the best overall
trade-off: Chamfer $0.968 \pm 0.078$~mm, Hausdorff $1.576 \pm 0.094$~mm,

This is the configuration used for all main results.
Distribution quality is further improved by the gauge-equivariant encoder
variants described in Section~\ref{sec:gauge_ablation}, which incorporate
per-vertex curvature features for anisotropic message passing.

\subsection{Gauge-Equivariant Encoder Ablation}
\label{sec:gauge_ablation}

We investigate whether the curvature-aware anisotropic message-passing
scheme described in Section~\ref{sec:gauge_method} improves shape
generation quality over the isotropic EGNN baseline.
All encoder variants below use the same four-layer structure and are
paired with the same flow matching velocity field; only the encoder
backbone changes.
Empirical equivariance error of the Gauge-EGNN encoder is $<4\times
10^{-7}$ under random $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ rotations, confirming that the
anisotropy weighting does not break SE(3)-equivariance.

\paragraph{Gauge-EGNN, $\beta{=}10^{-3}$ (no warmup).}
Substituting the encoder while keeping all other hyperparameters fixed
yields a modest reconstruction penalty (+0.05~mm Chamfer, +0.09~mm
Hausdorff) but raises the pathology probe accuracy from $32\%$ to $38\%$
($+6$ percentage points), confirming that curvature-aware anisotropic
message passing produces a more disease-discriminative latent space.

\paragraph{Gauge-EGNN + KL warmup.}
Pairing the Gauge-EGNN encoder with $\beta{=}5{\times}10^{-3}$ and 50-epoch
linear KL warmup recovers nearly all reconstruction quality (Chamfer
$1.004$~mm, Hausdorff $1.627$~mm) while substantially improving generation
quality: 1-NNA drops to $\mathbf{0.590}$ (the best across all tested
configurations) and EF error falls from $230\%$ to $\mathbf{157\%}$.
The EF improvement suggests that a more structured latent space leads to
more clinically coherent generated ED/ES pairs.
This configuration provides the best balance of reconstruction, generation,
and clinical validity.

\paragraph{Gauge-EGNN + geometric pooling + attention pooling.}
We augment the best gauge configuration with two pooling improvements:
(i) a per-vertex distance-to-centroid scalar derived from the refined
coordinates output by the GaugeEGNN (which were previously discarded),
concatenated to the node features before global pooling; and
(ii) an attention-weighted pool alongside the existing mean and max
pools, using per-node logits from a linear layer with scatter-softmax
normalisation.
The pathology probe improves to $\mathbf{41\%}$ ($+3$ pp over the
previous best), suggesting that retaining geometric refinement information
and weighting nodes by local structural importance yields a more
disease-discriminative latent code.
However, Chamfer and Hausdorff degrade slightly ($+0.02$~mm and
$+0.07$~mm respectively), and EF error rises sharply to $281\%$,
indicating that the richer pooling disturbs the volumetric coherence
of the latent space.
This variant is therefore most useful when probe accuracy is the
primary objective; the warmup-only configuration remains preferable
for clinical fidelity.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our results demonstrate that $\SE$-equivariance is a practically valuable
inductive bias for mesh-based cardiac shape modelling.
Comparing our EGNN-based model against the non-equivariant MLP flow
matching baseline (B4) on reconstruction quality
(Table~\ref{tab:reconstruction}), the equivariant model reduces Chamfer
distance by 8.8\% (0.968 vs.\ 1.060~mm) and Hausdorff distance by 8.5\%
(1.576 vs.\ 1.717~mm) on the full training set.
On distribution quality, it also achieves better 1-NNA (0.600 vs.\ 0.605)
and pathology probe accuracy (33\% vs.\ 31\%), confirming that equivariance
benefits both geometric fidelity and latent space structure.
The notably strong reconstruction performance of the Voxel diffusion
baseline (Chamfer 0.196~mm) reflects the advantages of operating on a
regular grid with dense supervision; however, this metric measures
voxel-to-voxel fidelity rather than surface geometry and is not directly
comparable to mesh-based Chamfer distances computed on 2{,}500-vertex
surfaces.

The most significant open issue is the clinical EF error (229.8\%),
which indicates that the current model does not reliably preserve
end-diastolic to end-systolic volume ratios.
This is expected at 200 training epochs without explicit volume
constraints; the flow-matching objective optimises geometric reconstruction
rather than functional cardiac mechanics.
Future work should incorporate volume-preserving regularisation or a
myocardial incompressibility term directly in the loss to improve
physiological plausibility.
The pathology probe accuracy of 33\% is above chance but well below
the linear separability one would want for clinical conditioning.
This suggests the latent space would benefit from semi-supervised training
or contrastive objectives that explicitly align latent clusters with
known pathology classes.

The gauge-equivariant encoder ablation (Section~\ref{sec:gauge_ablation})
reveals a clear pathway for improvement on both of these fronts.
Replacing the isotropic EGNN encoder with a curvature-aware anisotropic
variant improves pathology probe accuracy by 6 percentage points
($32\% \to 38\%$); further incorporating geometric coordinate pooling and
attention-weighted aggregation raises this to $41\%$.
Pairing the gauge encoder with KL warmup reduces EF error from $230\%$
to $157\%$, suggesting that a more structured latent space leads to
ED/ES pairs with more physiologically coherent volume ratios even without
explicit volume supervision.
These two improvements expose a trade-off: the geo+attn pooling variant
maximises probe accuracy but hurts EF fidelity, while the warmup-only
configuration provides the best clinical validity.
The consistent probe gain across all gauge variants indicates that
curvature features supply disease-morphology signal not captured by
vertex positions alone.

Our model has several limitations.
First, we train and evaluate on a single cardiac structure (LV) and two
time points (ED and ES), whereas a clinically useful model should cover
all four chambers across the full cardiac cycle.
Second, template registration is performed with rigid ICP, which assumes
that inter-subject shape differences can be decomposed into a rigid
alignment followed by a non-rigid deformation; this may fail for severe
pathologies where the overall heart geometry departs substantially from
the template.
Third, the ACDC dataset comprises only 100 subjects, limiting the
diversity of training shapes; performance on out-of-distribution
pathologies or paediatric populations is unknown.

Future directions include: (i)~a multi-structure joint model covering
LV, RV, and myocardium simultaneously; (ii)~4D temporal modelling by
conditioning the velocity field on cardiac phase; (iii)~learned
deformable registration to replace the rigid ICP alignment; and
(iv)~incorporating pathology labels as soft supervision to improve
latent space interpretability.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented an $\SE$-equivariant flow matching model for generative
cardiac shape modelling on surface meshes, combining an EGNN encoder with
an OT-conditional flow matching velocity field trained end-to-end on the
ACDC dataset.
On reconstruction, the model achieves Chamfer distance $0.968 \pm 0.078$~mm
and Hausdorff distance $1.576 \pm 0.094$~mm, an $8.7\times$ improvement
over the PCA shape model baseline.
Generated shapes attain a 1-NNA score of 0.600 and a pathology probe
accuracy of 33\%, demonstrating that the latent space captures
disease-relevant shape variation without label supervision.
Ablations confirm that $\SE$-equivariance, KL warmup, and a deeper
flow model each contribute meaningfully to the final performance.
Gauge-equivariant encoders augmented with surface curvature features
further improve distribution quality: the best configuration achieves
1-NNA $0.590$ and pathology probe $41\%$.
Pairing the gauge encoder with KL warmup reduces clinical EF error to
$157\%$ at a modest reconstruction cost, while geometric pooling
improvements trade EF fidelity for a further probe gain, revealing a
meaningful clinical--discriminative trade-off.
Together these results establish curvature-informed anisotropic message
passing as the strongest encoder family tested.
These results establish equivariant flow matching on meshes as a viable
and geometrically principled approach to generative cardiac shape
modelling, with clear pathways for improvement through volume-preserving
constraints, multi-structure modelling, and semi-supervised latent
space regularisation.
